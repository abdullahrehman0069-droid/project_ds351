Chapter 01 — Research Question & Motivation

Why Does
Price Divide
The Community?

The video game industry generated over $184 billion in 2023, yet player satisfaction is increasingly fragmented. Major publishers like EA, Activision, and Ubisoft routinely charge $60–$70 for titles that launch with technical problems, live-service paywalls, and incomplete content — sparking viral review-bombing campaigns.

Meanwhile, small indie studios charging under $25 consistently earn "Overwhelmingly Positive" ratings on Steam. This raises a compelling empirical question: Is there a statistically significant inverse relationship between game price and player satisfaction on Steam — and if so, which genres and price tiers drive this paradox?

Key Hypothesis Higher price → higher player expectation → lower satisfaction tolerance. Games charging premium prices face a punishing review environment when they fail to deliver polished, complete experiences on launch day.
Chapter 02 — The Pricing Landscape

Where The
Money Lives

Not all genres price equally. Racing and Action titles demand a premium entry fee — often $60–$70 for base game access, with additional DLC costs stacking on top. Meanwhile, Indie and Casual titles cluster below $20, frequently offering more complete, polished experiences at launch.

Chapter 05 — Verdict

The Data
Has Spoken

After analyzing hundreds of Steam titles across six genres and three price tiers, three structural truths emerge from the telemetry. These aren't opinions — they're patterns embedded in the data.

💸

PREMIUM TRAP

Games priced $60+ face a satisfaction ceiling of ~72%. Players paying premium prices punish technical failures and incomplete launches far more harshly than budget buyers.

🎯

INDIE ADVANTAGE

Sub-$25 indie titles consistently achieve 85–92% satisfaction. Tight scope, transparent development, and complete out-of-the-box experiences build community loyalty that marketing budgets cannot buy.

⚡

FATIGUE IS REAL

High-player-count live-service titles show the highest satisfaction variance. Initial hype drives peak metrics, but server instability and aggressive monetization erode approval over time.
